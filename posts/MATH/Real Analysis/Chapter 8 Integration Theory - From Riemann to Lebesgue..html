Definition - 0:

A partition is "cutting up interval into n smaller ones".

  • Upper sum ≥ Riemann sum.
  • Riemann sum ≥ Lower sum. We want the best upper and lower sum which means thet both equal the Riemann integral.

Definition - 1:

Let f:[a,b]→R be a bounded function (function with bounded value).

  • A partition of [a,b] is finite subset P={x0,x1,...,xn−1,xn} such that a=x0<x1<x2<...<xn=b
  • Given a bounded function f:[a,b]→R(a<b) and a partition P of [a,b],
    • Upper sum of f with respect to P: $$U(f,P):=\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}M_{i}(x_{i}-x_{i-1})$$ where Mi=sup{f(x):x∈[xi−1,xi]}
    • Upper sum of f on [a,b]: $$U(f):=\inf_{P}U(f,P)=\inf{U(f,P)\in \mathbb{R}😛 }$$ U(f): min of max sum
    • Lower sum of f with respect to P: $$L(f,P):=\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}m_{i}(x_{i}-x_{i-1})$$where mi=inf{f(x):x∈[xi−1,xi]}
    • Lower sum of f on [a,b]: $$L(f):=\sup_{P}L(f,P)$$ L(f): max of min sum.
    • Max of min ≤ min of max
  • Let M=supx∈[a,b]f(x),m=infx∈[a,b]f(x), then $$m(b-a)\leq L(f,P)\leq U(f,P)\leq M(b-a)$$
  • Refinig P to P′, i.e. P⊃P′, we have:![[Pasted image 20230409171033.png]]With finer partitions, piecewise constant functions approximate f better, upper bound improves (decrease), lower bound improves (increases).

Definition - 2:

If U(f)=L(f), then we say:

  • f is Riemann integrable; denote f(x)∈R[a,b].
  • The Riemann integral of f is ∫abf(x)dx:=U(f)=L(f).

Riemann's criterion:

Bounded f(x) is Riemann integrable on [a,b] if and only if ∀ϵ>0,∃P of [a,b] sucth that $$U(f,P)-L(f,P)=\sum\limits_{i=1}^{n}(M_{i}-m_{i})(x_{i}-x_{i-1})<\epsilon$$

Theorem - 1

Any continuous function on [a,b] is Riemann integrable. More formally, f∈C0[a,b]⇒f∈R[a,b]. Even more formally, C0[a,b]⊃R[a,b].

Proof

Theorem - 2: Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

(a). Suppose F:[a,b]→R is differentiable, and f=F′:[a,b]→R is Riemann integrable. Then, $$\int_{a}^{b}f(x)dx = \int_{a}^{b}F'(x)dx=F(b)-F(a)$$ (b). Suppose f:[a,b]→R is continuous. Define F(x):=∫axf(t)dt. Then F(x) is differentiable, with $$\frac{dF}{dx}(x)=f(x)$$

Linearity of Riemann Integral

Proposition - 1.

Assume f,g are integrable on [a,b], and c∈R. Then,

  1. c⋅f(x) and f(x)+g(x) are integrable.
    • In other word, R[a,b] forms a vector space
  2. ∫abc⋅f(x)dx=c⋅∫abf(x)dx,∫ab(f(x)+g(x))dx=∫abf(x)dx+∫abg(x)dx
    • In other words, ∫ab(−)dx:R[a,b]→R is a linear map.

Proof
